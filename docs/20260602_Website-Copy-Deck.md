## What Is RCV Page

SEO title: What Is Ranked-Choice Voting? | Melrose RCV

SEO description: A plain-language explanation of ranked-choice voting, instant-runoff voting, and how ranked ballots differ from choose-one elections.

Primary CTA: See how RCV works

Secondary CTA: Read common questions

Draft copy:

Ranked-choice voting, often called RCV, is a way to vote when there are multiple candidates. Instead of choosing only one candidate, voters can rank candidates in the order they prefer: first choice, second choice, third choice, and so on. [source: `ncsl-rcv`]

The basic idea is simple: your first choice is counted first. Later choices are backup choices. In a single-winner RCV election, a later choice can matter if an earlier-ranked candidate is eliminated during the count. [source: `mit-irv`]

"Ranked-choice voting" is an umbrella term. It can describe different systems that use ranked ballots. For a single-winner office, RCV is often counted as instant-runoff voting. For multi-winner offices, ranked ballots may be counted with proportional representation or another multi-winner method. Cambridge, Massachusetts uses proportional representation for City Council and School Committee elections. That is a useful Massachusetts example, but it is not the same thing as a single-winner instant-runoff election. [source: `cambridge-election`]

RCV does not automatically decide which Melrose offices would be covered, how many rankings voters would have, whether at-large offices would use the same method as single-seat offices, or how recounts and audits would work. Those questions would need legal and election-administration review before any public proposal is final.

Supporters argue RCV gives voters a way to rank a favorite candidate and backup choices without having to choose only one. They also argue it can reduce some vote-splitting pressure in crowded races. Those are reasons to consider RCV, not guarantees about every election outcome.

## How It Works Page

SEO title: How Ranked-Choice Voting Works | Melrose RCV

SEO description: Learn how ranked-choice voting counts first choices, transfers ballots in rounds, and handles backup rankings.

Primary CTA: See why Melrose might consider RCV

Secondary CTA: Read myths and facts

Draft copy:

This page explains single-winner ranked-choice voting, also called instant-runoff voting. It is a general explanation, not an official Melrose ballot instruction. Melrose-specific ballot design, rank limits, write-in rules, skipped rankings, overvotes, recounts, audits, and tabulation software would need election-administration and legal review. [source: `ncsl-implementation`]

On a ranked ballot, a voter can mark a first choice, second choice, third choice, and additional choices if the ballot allows. A voter usually does not have to rank every candidate, but ranking fewer candidates can affect whether the ballot has a continuing choice in later rounds. [source: `boulder-rcv`]

The count starts with first-choice votes. If a candidate reaches the winning threshold under the rules, that candidate wins. In many single-winner explanations, the threshold is a majority of active ballots in the final round, but the exact denominator must be described carefully because ballots can become inactive or exhausted. [source: `mit-irv`]

If no candidate reaches the threshold, the candidate with the fewest votes is eliminated. Ballots that were counting for that candidate move to the next ranked candidate on each ballot who is still continuing. A ballot counts for one continuing candidate at a time. Ranking a second choice does not take support away from a first choice while that first choice remains in the count. [sources: `mit-irv`, `nyc-votes-rcv`]

If all candidates ranked on a ballot have been eliminated, or if no valid next ranking remains under the rules, that ballot may become exhausted or inactive in later rounds. That does not mean the ballot never counted. It means it no longer has a continuing candidate to count for. [source: `ncsl-implementation`]

Ranked ballots need clear instructions. Voters should be able to understand how to rank, how to avoid common marking mistakes, and what happens if they skip a ranking or mark the same candidate more than once. Those rules vary by jurisdiction, so Melrose should not copy another city's ballot instructions without official review. [source: `center-civic-design-rcv`]

## Home Rule Petition Page

SEO title: Massachusetts Home Rule Petition Path For Melrose RCV

SEO description: Learn why a Melrose ranked-choice voting proposal would need legal review, local civic action, and likely state legislative authorization.

Primary CTA: Ask a question or get involved

Secondary CTA: Read sources

Draft copy:

Melrose has not adopted ranked-choice voting. A realistic path would likely require local civic action and state authorization through a Massachusetts home rule petition. This page explains the working theory, not final legal advice.

In Massachusetts, a home rule petition is a local request for the state Legislature to pass a special law for a city or town. Article LXXXIX of the Massachusetts Constitution describes how the General Court may act by special laws relating to a particular municipality, including laws based on locally filed or approved petitions. [source: `ma-constitution`]

The research indicates Melrose likely needs state authorization because the Home Rule Amendment does not give cities and towns broad power to regulate elections beyond specific charter procedures. That makes election-method changes a legal-review issue, not something the campaign should present as a simple local switch. [sources: `ma-constitution`, `ma-ch43b`]

A Melrose petition would likely ask the state to authorize ranked-choice voting for specified Melrose municipal elections. The petition text would need to decide or enable office scope, counting method, treatment of multi-seat offices, any local voter-approval step, charter interaction, state election law interaction, and implementation authority. Those are legal and election-administration questions.

Likely pathway, subject to legal review:

1. Residents and local officials define the proposal and review the law.
2. Petition language is drafted with municipal and election-law review.
3. The Melrose City Council and any other required local actors consider the petition.
4. If locally approved for filing, Melrose's state representative or senator may file it as a bill.
5. The bill goes through the Massachusetts legislative process, including committee review.
6. If passed by the Legislature and approved by the Governor through the proper process, the special act provides the authority it states.
7. Melrose would still need implementation planning before any first RCV election.

Boston's RCV home rule petition is a useful Massachusetts example of a local proposal moving to the Legislature, but Boston's process and text do not automatically apply to Melrose. [sources: `ma-h4262-boston`, `boston-rcv`]

Legal and election-administration disclaimer: this page is not legal advice. Final petition language, local procedure, state legislative strategy, ballot design, tabulation, audits, recounts, voter education, and timing require review by appropriate legal, municipal, and election-administration authorities.

## Myths And Facts Page

SEO title: Ranked-Choice Voting Myths, Facts, And Concerns

SEO description: A respectful guide to common RCV misconceptions, real implementation concerns, and what Melrose would still need to verify.

Primary CTA: Read the FAQ

Secondary CTA: Check the sources

Draft copy:

RCV questions deserve calm answers. Some claims are simply wrong, some are overpromises, and some are real implementation concerns that Melrose would need to take seriously.

"RCV lets some people vote more than once." Fact: in single-winner RCV, each ballot counts for one continuing candidate at a time. If a candidate is eliminated, that ballot may transfer to the next ranked continuing candidate. The transfer is not an extra vote. [source: `mit-irv`]

"Ranking a second choice hurts my first choice." Fact: a later ranking is a backup choice. It does not count against your first choice while your first choice remains in the count. [source: `boulder-rcv`]

"RCV is too confusing." Concern: RCV is different from choose-one voting, and voters deserve clear education. The right response is plain-language materials, accessible ballot design, practice examples, and careful official instructions. [source: `center-civic-design-rcv`]

"RCV guarantees better candidates." Overclaim: no voting method can guarantee better candidates. RCV may change campaign incentives in some settings, but research on campaign tone and outcomes is mixed.

"RCV is a silver bullet." Overclaim: RCV is one possible reform. It does not replace good public service, candidate recruitment, campaign transparency, civic participation, or competent election administration.

"RCV always takes too long to count." Concern: RCV can require more explanation and round-by-round reporting. Timing depends on local rules, mail ballots, equipment, close contests, and certification requirements. Melrose should verify result reporting before any proposal becomes public voter instruction. [source: `ncsl-implementation`]

"RCV is impossible to audit." Concern: RCV audits and recounts depend on equipment, cast vote records, tabulation software, and state/local rules. The site should not claim audits are solved until election administrators and counsel review the plan. [source: `rcvrc`]

"The candidate with the most first-choice votes should always win." Civic judgment question: plurality elections emphasize first choices. Single-winner RCV asks whether a winner should also survive runoff-style rounds if no candidate reaches the threshold at the start.

"Melrose can just switch to RCV on its own." Fact with legal-review caveat: the research indicates Melrose likely needs state authorization because Massachusetts municipalities do not have broad local authority over election regulation. The exact path requires legal review. [source: `ma-constitution`]
