# RCV Website Benchmark Research

Date accessed: 2026-06-02

Purpose: benchmark leading ranked-choice voting (RCV) education, election-administration, advocacy, and civic-design resources so future Melrose RCV website work can adapt strong patterns without copying language, layouts, or claims.

This document is research only. It is not final website copy.

## Evaluation Lens

- Homepage hook: what promise or problem the page starts with.
- Visual design: whether the page uses ballots, diagrams, videos, practice tools, or plain text.
- Navigation: whether residents can quickly find "what is it," "how do I vote," "where is it used," "FAQs," and "take action."
- Explanation clarity: whether the page avoids jargon, explains instant-runoff counting, and states which elections use RCV.
- Trust-building: whether the page is official, nonpartisan, transparent about limits, or includes implementation details.
- Accessibility and plain language: whether the page includes multilingual materials, accessible ballot guidance, or simple step-by-step instructions.
- Weaknesses to avoid: places where claims are too broad, too advocacy-heavy, too technical, or not close enough to source support.

## FairVote

Source: https://fairvote.org/our-reforms/ranked-choice-voting-information/

Publisher type: national RCV advocacy and research organization.

### What Works

- Strong movement hook: FairVote frames RCV as giving voters more choice and more voice.
- Broad FAQ structure covers practical resident questions: ranking only one candidate, whether ranking a second choice hurts a first choice, costs, candidate behavior, where RCV is used, and administration.
- Useful implementation sections connect to ballot design, results reporting, voting systems, and audits.
- Trust-building through external resource links to Center for Civic Design, RCV Resource Center, audit resources, and results-reporting guidance.
- Good plain-language correction of a common fear: ranking another candidate does not affect a first choice unless the first choice is eliminated.

### Weaknesses To Avoid

- Benefits are framed confidently from an advocacy perspective. A Melrose site should not repeat claims such as "more civil campaigns," "more diverse winners," "saves money," or "majority rule" without precise sourcing and caveats.
- Cost discussion includes examples that may not transfer to Melrose because local equipment, ballot layout, administrative staffing, and state law may differ.
- "Where used" data can change frequently and should be treated as a living source, not hard-coded without a current check.

### Ideas Worth Adapting

- A resident-first FAQ that starts with practical concerns.
- A dedicated "What happens if I rank only one candidate?" explanation.
- A separate implementation page for administration, audits, reporting, equipment, and voter education.
- Links to technical resources kept near implementation claims.

## Ranked Choice Voting Resource Center

Source: https://www.rcvresources.org/

Publisher type: nonpartisan nonprofit focused on RCV implementation resources.

### What Works

- Strong trust posture for administrators: the site identifies itself as a nonpartisan 501(c)(3) resource and emphasizes implementation expertise.
- Navigation is organized around election lifecycle needs: pre-election, election day, post-election, ballot layout, tabulation, results, audits, and "RCV in a Box."
- Especially useful for Melrose because it is not merely persuasive; it documents practical details future local officials would need to evaluate.
- The "RCV in a Box" concept suggests a good mental model for an implementation readiness checklist.

### Weaknesses To Avoid

- The site is more of a library than a resident-facing story. A Melrose public website should be easier to scan and more local.
- Some material assumes a reader already knows election administration vocabulary.

### Ideas Worth Adapting

- Create a "What election officials would need to review" page with neutral categories: ballot design, voter education, poll worker training, tabulation, reporting, audits, accessibility, and costs.
- Use RCVRC as a source log anchor for technical claims, not as the sole civic argument for adoption.

## Maine Secretary of State

Source: https://www.maine.gov/sos/elections-voting/resources-for-ranked-choice-voting

Publisher type: official state election administration.

### What Works

- High-trust official source for actual use of RCV in Maine.
- Uses explainer animation, marked-ballot examples, rules, recount procedures, and a timeline of legislative/legal history.
- Separates voter instructions from legal and administrative materials.
- Demonstrates that legal history and implementation scope can be complicated; Maine uses RCV in some elections and not all elections.

### Weaknesses To Avoid

- Government-resource pages can be utilitarian and dense.
- Maine's legal framework is not Massachusetts's legal framework. Do not imply Maine's implementation path maps onto Melrose.

### Ideas Worth Adapting

- Use a "scope of use" box: exactly which elections would change and which would not.
- Maintain a legal-history/timeline page only after the Melrose path is verified.
- Include example ballots and a "properly mark the ballot" resource once election officials or legal counsel approve the local design.

## NYC Votes / NYC Campaign Finance Board

Source: https://www.nycvotes.org/rcv

Publisher type: official nonpartisan voter engagement initiative of the New York City Campaign Finance Board.

### What Works

- Excellent resident-facing hook: "rank up to five candidates ... instead of choosing just one."
- Clearly states scope: NYC uses RCV only in primary and special elections for city offices, not general elections or state/national offices.
- Strong visual strategy: video, ballot illustration, mock election, practice ballot, and top FAQs.
- Plain-language reassurance: voters may rank one candidate, do not need to rank five, and can ask poll workers for help or a new ballot if they make a mistake.
- Good "practice ranking" pattern lowers anxiety before explaining every technical detail.

### Weaknesses To Avoid

- Benefit claims about more diverse winners should be cited carefully and framed as "studies or advocates report," not as a guaranteed outcome.
- NYC's five-rank limit and office scope are specific to NYC law; Melrose should not borrow those details without local legal/admin review.

### Ideas Worth Adapting

- A practice ballot or mock ranking exercise would be one of the highest-value future UX elements.
- Put "Which Melrose elections would this affect?" close to the top.
- Include a "Do I have to rank everyone?" FAQ.

## NYC Board of Elections

Source: https://vote.nyc/RankedChoiceVoting

Publisher type: official election administrator.

### What Works

- Builds trust by citing the 2019 City Charter amendment and listing exact offices.
- Counting explanation is brief and administrative: count first choices, elect if more than 50%, otherwise continue in rounds.
- Official source is useful to pair with NYC Votes: one page teaches, the other establishes administrative authority.

### Weaknesses To Avoid

- Less engaging than NYC Votes for residents new to the subject.
- Does not fully explore concerns or tradeoffs.

### Ideas Worth Adapting

- Pair any advocacy/campaign page with an "official process and authority" page so residents can distinguish campaign goals from government action.

## Cambridge, Massachusetts Election Commission

Source: https://www.cambridgema.gov/Departments/ElectionCommission

Publisher type: official municipal election administrator.

### What Works

- Extremely relevant Massachusetts precedent because Cambridge uses proportional representation (a ranked voting method) for City Council and School Committee elections.
- The Election Commission page directly offers a way to learn how Cambridge votes in local elections and try a practice ranked choice ballot.
- Useful example of longstanding local ranked-ballot administration in Massachusetts.

### Weaknesses To Avoid

- Cambridge's proportional representation system is not the same as single-winner instant-runoff RCV.
- Future Melrose copy must explain the difference between single-winner RCV for offices like mayor/ward councilor and multi-winner methods for at-large bodies.

### Ideas Worth Adapting

- Explain "ranked-choice voting" as an umbrella term before discussing which counting method Melrose might seek.
- Use Cambridge as a Massachusetts context example only with careful wording, not as proof that Melrose can implement automatically.

## Minneapolis Elections

Source: https://vote.minneapolismn.gov/ranked-choice-voting/details/

Publisher type: official municipal election administrator.

### What Works

- Strong information architecture: "Understanding RCV," "Practice using ranked choice voting," and "Common questions."
- Clear local scope: Minneapolis uses RCV for mayor, city council, Board of Estimate and Taxation, and Park and Recreation Board, but not school board or county/state/federal offices.
- Uses simple steps for marking a ballot and explains that later choices are used only if earlier choices are eliminated.

### Weaknesses To Avoid

- Some local details, such as a three-rank ballot, are jurisdiction-specific.

### Ideas Worth Adapting

- Melrose should have a tight scope box modeled on Minneapolis: "would affect / would not affect."
- A future site should keep "how to mark a ballot" separate from "how votes are counted."

## Portland and Multnomah County, Oregon

Sources:

- https://www.portland.gov/vote/ranked-choice-voting
- https://multco.us/info/ranked-choice-voting-rcv

Publisher type: official city and county election/voter education.

### What Works

- Portland gives a direct "what changed" explanation and distinguishes single-winner RCV from multi-winner RCV.
- Multnomah County provides multilingual access links and a clear "when voters will use RCV" section.
- The county page links to an implementation report, which is a strong trust-building device.
- Good example of coordinating city voter education with county election administration.

### Weaknesses To Avoid

- Portland's transition combined a new form of government, multi-member districts, and RCV; that context is much more complex than a narrow Melrose municipal-election proposal might be.
- Media coverage of Portland's first RCV election raised concerns about voter drop-off and ballot burden in very crowded races. A Melrose site should acknowledge that candidate volume and ballot complexity matter.

### Ideas Worth Adapting

- Include "single-winner vs multi-winner" explainers.
- Include multilingual and accessibility planning as implementation topics, not afterthoughts.
- Treat first-election evaluation as part of the plan: what data should be reviewed after an RCV election?

## Boulder County / City of Boulder

Source: https://bouldercounty.gov/elections/information/ranked-choice-voting/

Publisher type: official county election administrator.

### What Works

- Excellent ballot-marking guidance with examples of common errors: duplicate rankings, multiple candidates in a column, and skipped rankings.
- Explains the local adoption story: voters approved direct mayoral election through RCV after a charter amendment petition.
- Includes an interactive practice ballot and webinars.
- Explicitly warns that later rankings are only used if higher-ranked candidates are eliminated.

### Weaknesses To Avoid

- Some ballot-error rules, such as treatment of skipped rankings, are Colorado-specific and must not be imported into Melrose without Massachusetts/local election review.

### Ideas Worth Adapting

- "Common ballot errors" should be part of future voter education.
- Adoption history should be told as a process with votes and legal steps, not as an automatic policy switch.

## Alameda County / Oakland-Berkeley-San Leandro

Source: https://acvote.alamedacountyca.gov/voting/rcv

Publisher type: official county registrar.

### What Works

- Strong multilingual presentation and disability-access navigation.
- Explains which offices use RCV and which do not: local officials, not county/state/federal offices or ballot measures.
- Clear grid-ballot explanation.
- Shows correct and incorrect markings.

### Weaknesses To Avoid

- The page is administrative and visually crowded.
- Five-rank limit and affected cities are local facts only.

### Ideas Worth Adapting

- Future Melrose education should include multilingual and accessible versions early.
- The "what RCV does not apply to" section is essential for preventing overclaiming.

## Center for Civic Design

Source: https://civicdesign.org/topics/rcv/

Publisher type: nonpartisan civic-design research organization.

### What Works

- Best source for UX principles rather than RCV advocacy claims.
- Frames voter education around voter intent: residents deserve information and ballot design that help them vote as intended and understand how votes are counted.
- Provides separate tools for voter education, ballot layout, results display, news-article display, and accessible RCV ballots.

### Weaknesses To Avoid

- The resource is design guidance, not Melrose legal authority.

### Ideas Worth Adapting

- Build future visual explanations around voter intent, not persuasion.
- Treat election results display as a voter-education issue before election day.
- Do accessibility review for ballot examples and diagrams before publishing.

## Balanced and Critical Sources

Sources:

- NCSL overview: https://www.ncsl.org/elections-and-campaigns/ranked-choice-voting
- NCSL implementation considerations: https://www.ncsl.org/elections-and-campaigns/ranked-choice-voting-in-practice-implementation-considerations-for-policymakers
- MIT Election Lab IRV overview: https://electionlab.mit.edu/research/instant-runoff-voting
- MIT Election Lab cost article: https://electionlab.mit.edu/articles/cost-ranked-choice-voting
- Pettigrew and Radley paper: https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4670677

### What Works

- NCSL is useful for current legal landscape and implementation considerations.
- MIT Election Lab gives a sober research posture: RCV/IRV is legitimate, but evidence on benefits is mixed or still developing.
- Cost and ballot-error research warns against simplistic claims.
- Pettigrew/Radley identifies mismarking and differential impact risks that a responsible Melrose site should not hide.

### Weaknesses To Avoid

- Some research is technical and can be misread by residents if not translated carefully.
- Criticism pages sometimes use charged terms such as "disenfranchised"; future copy should explain exhausted ballots neutrally and precisely.

### Ideas Worth Adapting

- Include a "Questions worth asking" page that treats skepticism as civic diligence.
- Avoid guarantees. Prefer: "Advocates argue," "research suggests," "studies are mixed," "implementation depends on..."
- Explain exhausted ballots, skipped rankings, overvotes, tabulation timing, and results transparency in plain language.

## Overall Benchmark Findings

- Best pages are local, specific, and explicit about which elections use RCV.
- Official pages build trust by showing legal authority, ballot examples, practice tools, and election-office contacts.
- Advocacy pages are strongest for motivation and FAQs but need balancing with official and research sources.
- Civic-design resources should guide the future site structure: plain-language steps, visual ballot examples, accessible diagrams, and results displays.
- The Melrose site should be beautiful but not decorative-first. The "beauty" should come from clarity, local trust, careful diagrams, and respect for residents who are undecided or skeptical.

## Patterns To Avoid

- Saying RCV "guarantees" majority support, civility, lower costs, diversity, or turnout.
- Using examples from another jurisdiction without labeling the jurisdiction and legal context.
- Hiding implementation tradeoffs below advocacy content.
- Failing to distinguish single-winner RCV, multi-winner proportional RCV, and Cambridge-style proportional representation.
- Treating legal authorization as a formality.
- Publishing ballot mockups without election-administration review.
