# Brand System

Date: 2026-06-02

Purpose: define the civic identity direction for Melrose RCV before final page copy, logo artwork, or polished visual design work.

This document is design guidance. It is not final public copy.

## Brand Position

Melrose RCV should feel like a neighborly civic education project: local, serious, calm, transparent, and practical. The identity should invite residents to learn before deciding, while making the campaign's organizing goal clear.

The brand should not feel like a national partisan campaign, a generic SaaS product, or a legal filing.

## Brand Attributes

- Civic.
- Warm.
- Local.
- Trustworthy.
- Nonpartisan.
- Modern.
- Clear.
- Accessible.
- Neighborly.
- Practical.
- Calm but purposeful.

## Brand Promise

Melrose residents can learn what ranked-choice voting is, understand the legal pathway, ask hard questions, and decide whether to support bringing RCV to Melrose municipal elections through a careful civic process.

## Voice And Tone

Use:

- Plain language.
- Short explanations.
- Respectful treatment of skepticism.
- "Supporters argue" for campaign claims.
- "Needs review" for unresolved legal or election-administration details.
- Local civic language.

Avoid:

- Partisan framing.
- Victory language.
- Fear-based appeals.
- National campaign slogans.
- Overconfident legal or election-administration claims.
- Dismissive "myth-busting" for valid concerns.

## Wordmark Direction

Recommended direction:

- Text-forward wordmark: "Melrose RCV."
- Small civic mark using an abstract ranked ballot stack or Main Street-inspired civic square.
- Avoid official City of Melrose seals, municipal insignia, or anything that implies government endorsement.
- Avoid checkmarks as the primary logo idea because they can imply official ballot instruction.

Current component direction:

- Header uses a compact "RCV" mark paired with "Melrose RCV" and "Melrose civic education."
- The mark is intentionally simple and reusable until a final logo is designed.

Future logo exploration:

- Ranked ballot columns rendered as simple vertical bars.
- A civic meeting-house or Main Street window grid abstracted into square geometry.
- A path/timeline symbol for the home rule petition process.

## Color Rationale

The palette avoids partisan red/blue pairings by using a grounded civic blue with green, gold, rust, lilac, paper, and ink neutrals.

Primary colors:

- Civic blue: trust, clarity, official-adjacent seriousness without using partisan bright blue.
- Civic green: community, local stewardship, volunteer action.
- Civic gold: attention, process notes, legal/source caution.

Supporting colors:

- Rust: warm emphasis for caution or myth/fact contrast.
- Lilac: legal/process review and neutral complexity.
- Paper and ink: readable civic-document warmth without academic austerity.

Avoid:

- Saturated red/blue campaign pairings.
- Dark blue/slate domination.
- Purple-blue gradients.
- Beige-only civic parchment.
- Decorative color effects without information purpose.

## Typography Direction

Recommended font strategy:

- Use a highly readable humanist sans-serif stack for interface and body copy.
- Prefer free web fonts if later loaded or self-hosted, such as Atkinson Hyperlegible or Inter.
- Use a restrained serif only as an optional accent for editorial callouts, not core navigation or small UI.

Current tokens:

- Sans stack begins with Atkinson Hyperlegible, then Inter, then system UI.
- Serif and mono stacks are available for future use.

Rationale:

- Atkinson Hyperlegible supports accessibility and readability.
- Inter/system UI keeps the site modern and practical.
- No condensed, novelty, or campaign-poster fonts.

## Visual Motifs

Use:

- Ballot grids.
- Rounded civic squares.
- Process paths.
- Thin rules and source tags.
- Calm status dots.
- Local civic structure through spacing and alignment.

Avoid:

- Clip art ballots.
- Waving flags.
- Stars and stripes motifs.
- Aggressive campaign badges.
- Megaphones.
- Dense election-night dashboards.
- Decorative blobs or gradient orbs.

## Diagram Style

Ballot diagrams:

- Use table/grid structure.
- Use fictional candidates only.
- Use one selected mark per rank column.
- Use text labels, not color-only meaning.
- Include review disclaimers.

Counting diagrams:

- Use step cards.
- Show sequence with small connector marks.
- Keep "one ballot counts for one continuing candidate at a time" as a core concept.

Home rule pathway diagrams:

- Use timeline/step cards with legal-review status.
- Avoid exact dates until verified.
- Show local and state steps as distinct.

## Illustration And Photo Direction

Recommended:

- Real Melrose civic/public-space photography only if rights are clear.
- Warm local scenes: civic meetings, Main Street context, public library/community spaces, ballot education workshops.
- Simple custom diagrams for RCV mechanics and home rule process.

Avoid:

- Stock handshake photos.
- National capitol imagery.
- Candidate or party imagery.
- Crowded ballot photography that suggests official instructions.
- Photos that imply official City of Melrose endorsement.

## CTA Hierarchy

Primary:

- Learn how RCV works.
- Join updates.
- Ask a question.

Secondary:

- Review the pathway.
- Check the sources.
- Volunteer.

Support:

- Donate/support once compliance is verified.

CTA tone:

- Civic and low-pressure.
- Actionable without overpromising.

Avoid:

- "Adopt RCV now."
- "Make every vote count."
- "Fix Melrose elections."
- "Guarantee majority winners."

## Common Brand Mistakes To Avoid

- Making the campaign look official.
- Making the site feel partisan.
- Overusing ballot imagery.
- Using national advocacy slogans as local brand voice.
- Treating legal review as a footnote.
- Presenting skepticism as ignorance.
- Letting beauty outrun source grounding.

## Brand Test

Before publishing future design work, ask:

- Would a skeptical resident feel respected?
- Would a local official see legal/process humility?
- Would a supporter know how to help?
- Would a source checker know where claims came from?
- Would the design still work if the user ignores color?
- Does anything imply Melrose has already adopted RCV?
