# Open Questions And Risks

Date accessed: 2026-06-02

Purpose: track legal, election-administration, civic, content, and misinformation risks for a future Melrose RCV website. This document is research only.

## Highest-Priority Risks

1. Overstating legal authority.
2. Implying Melrose has adopted RCV.
3. Treating implementation as automatic after advocacy support.
4. Using other jurisdictions' ballot rules as if they apply in Melrose.
5. Making uncited benefit claims.
6. Failing to explain exhausted ballots and implementation tradeoffs.
7. Publishing polished voter instructions before election officials or counsel review them.

## Legal Questions

Requires legal review:

- Does Melrose need a municipality-specific home rule petition, statewide local-option legislation, a charter amendment, or some combination?
- Who may initiate the local home rule petition process in Melrose?
- What local vote is required: City Council majority, supermajority, mayoral approval, voter petition, ballot question, or another process?
- Would a home rule petition need to include a local voter acceptance referendum after state passage?
- Can one petition cover mayor, ward councilors, councilors-at-large, and School Committee, or do different offices require different legal treatment?
- How should multi-seat at-large offices be handled legally?
- Would single-winner instant-runoff voting be lawful for all proposed single-seat municipal contests?
- Would proportional ranked-choice voting or another multi-winner ranked method be lawful for at-large seats?
- How would the Melrose Charter's current election provisions interact with any special act?
- Would the proposal amend the charter, create a special act outside the charter, or both?
- What Massachusetts General Laws governing local elections, ballots, nomination papers, preliminary elections, recounts, voting equipment, and certification would need to be referenced or exempted?
- Would implementation require approval or guidance from the Secretary of the Commonwealth?
- Are there equal-protection, accessibility, or ballot-access concerns that must be addressed in the petition text?
- Would a local advisory question be legally available or politically useful before filing?

Claims that must not be made without legal verification:

- "Melrose can adopt RCV by City Council vote."
- "A home rule petition is all that is needed."
- "The Legislature must approve a locally approved petition."
- "Melrose voters will definitely vote on RCV."
- "RCV would apply to all municipal offices."
- "The first RCV election could happen in [specific year]."

## Election Administration Questions

Requires official review with the City Clerk/Elections Office and possibly state election officials:

- What voting equipment and ballot scanners does Melrose use?
- Does current equipment support ranked ballots?
- Can the system export cast vote records suitable for RCV tabulation?
- Would tabulation be performed locally, by a vendor, by the state, or with separate software?
- Is any RCV tabulation software certified or acceptable under Massachusetts requirements?
- How many ranks could be offered on a Melrose ballot?
- How would the ballot fit if many candidates run?
- How would write-in candidates be handled?
- How would skipped rankings, repeated candidates, overvotes, undervotes, and blank rankings be handled?
- How would mail ballots and accessible voting systems present ranked choices?
- How would poll workers be trained?
- How much voter education would be needed before first use?
- What languages and accessible formats should be provided?
- How would preliminary results be reported?
- Would round-by-round results be available on election night?
- Would cast vote records be published, and if so with what privacy protections?
- How would recounts work?
- How would risk-limiting audits or other post-election audits work?
- What would implementation cost?
- Would RCV change staffing needs or election-night procedures?
- How would the campaign avoid confusing voters in non-RCV state/federal elections?

Claims that must not be made without election-administration verification:

- "Current equipment can already run RCV."
- "RCV would have little or no cost."
- "Results would be available on election night."
- "Audits and recounts would work the same way."
- "Voters could rank up to five candidates."
- "Skipped rankings would be counted in [specific way]."
- "A ballot with an overvote would be handled in [specific way]."

## Political And Civic Questions

Open questions:

- Is there existing support or opposition among Melrose residents, elected officials, civic groups, and election administrators?
- Which residents are most likely to be skeptical, and what questions do they have?
- Would the campaign be resident-led, official-led, or both?
- What local values should frame the campaign: voter choice, fair local races, civility, better handling of crowded races, avoiding vote splitting, or civic modernization?
- Are there recent Melrose election examples with crowded races or plurality outcomes that are relevant and sourceable?
- Would discussing specific past elections feel constructive or divisive?
- What concerns might arise around ballot complexity, cost, trust in results, or local political advantage?
- How can the campaign remain nonpartisan in a city where municipal races may still have informal political dynamics?
- What community partners would be trusted messengers?
- How should the campaign coordinate with official sources without blurring the line between advocacy and election administration?

Risks:

- Residents may see RCV as a national partisan issue rather than a local election-method proposal.
- Supporters may overpromise benefits.
- Opponents may overstate fraud, confusion, or disenfranchisement claims.
- Using past local races as examples could look like targeting specific candidates or factions.
- A campaign site could accidentally appear to speak for the City of Melrose.

Mitigations:

- Keep the site clearly nonpartisan.
- Label campaign content separately from official election information.
- Link residents to official City of Melrose and Massachusetts election resources.
- Use fictional examples for ballot diagrams.
- Include respectful concern-oriented content.

## Design And Content Risks

Risks:

- Diagrams could simplify counting so much that they become misleading.
- A beautiful interface could make unreviewed claims feel more authoritative than they are.
- "Myth vs fact" content could dismiss legitimate implementation concerns.
- A practice ballot could teach the wrong rules if not reviewed.
- National maps or "where used" counts could go stale quickly.
- Dense legal detail could overwhelm residents.
- Too much advocacy language could reduce trust with undecided voters.

Mitigations:

- Add "verified / interpretation / open question / legal-review placeholder" labels in internal docs and source logs.
- Keep citations close to claims.
- Build future diagrams from reviewed rules only.
- Include a "claims we are not making" page or section.
- Review all legal/process copy before launch.
- Keep source log current.

## Misinformation Risks

Potential misinformation or confusion:

- "RCV lets some people vote more than once."
- "RCV throws away votes."
- "Ranking a second choice hurts your first choice."
- "You must rank every candidate."
- "RCV always elects the majority favorite."
- "RCV always saves money."
- "RCV always increases turnout."
- "RCV guarantees more civil campaigns."
- "RCV is impossible to audit."
- "RCV is just a way to help one party."
- "Melrose already approved RCV."
- "A home rule petition bypasses voters."

Recommended response posture:

- Explain the mechanics neutrally.
- Acknowledge partial truths where they exist.
- Avoid ridicule.
- Use official sources for election rules.
- Use research sources for disputed empirical claims.

Example careful treatments:

- Some ballots can become exhausted if they have no remaining ranked continuing candidate; that is different from saying all such votes were never counted.
- A ballot counts for one continuing candidate at a time; transfer rounds are not extra votes.
- Ranking a backup choice does not affect a first choice while the first choice remains in the count.
- Cost effects depend on local context, equipment, staffing, voter education, and whether RCV replaces other elections.
- Auditability depends on equipment, cast vote records, procedures, and legal rules.

## Claims That Must Not Be Made Without Verification

Melrose status:

- Melrose has adopted ranked-choice voting.
- Melrose is implementing ranked-choice voting.
- Melrose voters will use RCV in the next municipal election.
- The City of Melrose supports this campaign.
- The Melrose Elections Office has approved any ballot design or explanation.

Legal pathway:

- A home rule petition guarantees RCV.
- Local approval is enough.
- State approval is a formality.
- The process will take a specific number of months.
- A specific state bill already authorizes Melrose.

Election mechanics:

- Melrose ballots will allow a specific number of rankings.
- Melrose will use single-winner RCV for all offices.
- Melrose will use proportional RCV for at-large offices.
- Results will be final on election night.
- Current voting machines are compatible.
- Recounts/audits are already solved.

Benefits:

- RCV guarantees majority winners.
- RCV eliminates spoilers in every case.
- RCV guarantees more civil campaigns.
- RCV guarantees higher turnout.
- RCV saves money.
- RCV makes elections more representative in Melrose.
- RCV reduces polarization in Melrose.

Opposition/concerns:

- All concerns about complexity are myths.
- RCV is too confusing for voters.
- RCV disenfranchises voters as a blanket claim.
- RCV is inherently fraudulent.
