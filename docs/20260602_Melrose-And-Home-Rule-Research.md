## Massachusetts Home Rule Petition Context

### Core Legal Framework

Primary sources:

- Massachusetts Constitution, Article LXXXIX / Home Rule Amendment: https://malegislature.gov/Laws/Constitution
- Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 43B, Home Rule Procedures: https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleVII/Chapter43B
- Secretary of the Commonwealth ballot-question petition page: https://www.sec.state.ma.us/divisions/elections/getting-on-the-ballot/petitioning-for-ballot-question.htm

Verified facts:

- Article LXXXIX gives cities and towns home rule powers but also reserves certain powers.
- Article LXXXIX Section 7 says the Home Rule Amendment does not grant cities and towns the power to regulate elections other than charter-adoption/revision/amendment procedures described in Sections 3 and 4.
- Article LXXXIX Section 8 describes how the General Court may act by special laws relating to a particular city or town, including laws enacted on a petition filed or approved by the voters, mayor and city council, other city legislative body, or town meeting.
- Chapter 43B is the Home Rule Procedures Act and covers adoption, revision, and amendment of municipal charters.
- The Secretary of the Commonwealth says only certain types of ballot questions authorized by law can be printed on a ballot, and local ballot questions require a state law or local charter allowing the petition process.

Interpretation:

- A Melrose RCV proposal likely needs legal review because election regulation is not a broad local home-rule power under Article LXXXIX.
- The likely pathway is a local request for special state legislation, commonly called a home rule petition.
- The exact local procedure in Melrose depends on the charter, City Council rules, state law, and the petition text.

### Likely Civic Path For A Melrose RCV Home Rule Petition

This is an interpretation based on Article LXXXIX, Massachusetts legislative examples, and other municipal RCV home rule examples. It requires legal review.

Possible steps:

1. Residents and/or local officials draft a proposed special act.
2. The proposal is reviewed for consistency with Massachusetts election law, the Melrose Charter, voting equipment constraints, and municipal powers.
3. The Melrose City Council considers whether to approve a home rule petition or other local measure.
4. If locally approved, Melrose's state representative or senator files the petition as a bill in the Massachusetts Legislature.
5. The bill is referred to a committee, likely Election Laws for an election-method proposal.
6. The committee may hold a hearing, receive testimony, amend, report, study, or otherwise act on the bill.
7. If passed by the Legislature and signed by the Governor, the special act would provide whatever authority its text grants.
8. Depending on the petition text and legal requirements, local voter acceptance, a future municipal ballot question, or additional implementation steps may be required.

### Massachusetts RCV-Related Legislative Examples

Sources:

- Local option bill S.531: https://malegislature.gov/Bills/194/S531
- Boston home rule petition H.4262: https://malegislature.gov/Bills/194/H4262
- Boston City Council article: https://www.boston.gov/node/16380706
- Bedford bill H.4916 committee list: https://malegislature.gov/Committees/Detail/J15/194/Bills/asc/EntityNumber/?current=false&pageNumber=1
- Bedford campaign/local source: https://www.bedfordrcv.org/

Verified facts:

- S.531 in the 194th General Court is titled "An Act providing a local option for ranked choice voting in municipal elections."
- S.531 was referred to the Joint Committee on Election Laws on February 27, 2025; a hearing was scheduled for November 13, 2025; and it was accompanied by a new draft, S.2911, on January 15, 2026.
- H.4262 is titled "An Act to implement ranked choice voting in Boston."
- H.4262 was filed by Representative Montaño of Boston with local approval received and referred to Election Laws.
- The H.4262 page shows a February 10, 2026 hearing and a May 11, 2026 action accompanying a study order.
- Boston's official City Council page says the Council voted 8-4 to advance a home rule petition for RCV in local elections, moving it to the Massachusetts Legislature for consideration, and says voters would see a future ballot question if it passes.
- The General Court committee list includes H.4916, "An Act authorizing ranked choice voting in the town of Bedford."

Caveats:

- BedfordRCV is a local advocacy/campaign source, not an official town or legislative source. It is useful for understanding campaign framing, but official town and legislative documents should be used for legal claims.
- The status of each bill can change and must be checked before public launch.
- Boston and Bedford examples do not prove Melrose can use the same text or pathway.
