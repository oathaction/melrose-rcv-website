# Content Governance And Claims Policy

Date: 2026-06-02

Purpose: define how the Melrose RCV website should handle factual claims, citations, uncertainty, legal review, election-administration review, donation claims, and future content updates.

This document is planning only, but it should govern future copywriting and implementation.

## Core Claim Rules

1. No unsourced factual claims.
2. No claim that Melrose has adopted RCV unless verified by official sources.
3. No claim that RCV is legally available without state authorization.
4. No claim that a home rule petition guarantees implementation.
5. No invented Melrose election facts.
6. No invented donation or compliance claims.
7. No partisan language.
8. Balanced treatment of concerns and implementation questions.

## Claim Categories

### Verified Fact

Definition:

- A statement supported by an official, reliable, or clearly identified source.

Examples:

- RCV lets voters rank candidates in order of preference.
- Melrose City Council has 11 members, with 4 at-large and 7 ward councilors.
- Article LXXXIX limits local authority to regulate elections beyond specified charter procedures.

Requirements:

- Cite source.
- Include access date in source log.
- Prefer official sources for law, municipal facts, and election administration.

### Campaign Interpretation

Definition:

- A campaign conclusion drawn from verified facts.

Examples:

- Melrose residents deserve clear information before deciding whether RCV belongs here.
- RCV is worth considering because it may reduce vote-splitting pressure in multi-candidate races.

Requirements:

- Label as supporter/campaign framing where appropriate.
- Do not present as guaranteed fact.
- Pair with concerns where the issue is contested.

### Open Question

Definition:

- A factual, legal, administrative, or civic issue not yet resolved.

Examples:

- Which Melrose offices would be included?
- Does current voting equipment support ranked ballots?
- Would local voter acceptance be required?

Requirements:

- Label clearly.
- Do not resolve by assumption.
- Track in open questions/risk docs.

### Legal-Review Placeholder

Definition:

- Any claim requiring review by qualified Massachusetts municipal/election counsel.

Examples:

- Exact home rule petition process.
- Whether one petition can cover all offices.
- Whether at-large offices can use a particular ranked method.

Requirements:

- Mark as requiring legal review.
- Do not publish final public copy as settled law.

### Election-Administration Placeholder

Definition:

- Any claim requiring confirmation from election officials or official election-administration materials.

Examples:

- Ballot design.
- Number of rankings.
- Tabulation software.
- Recounts and audits.
- Results timing.

Requirements:

- Mark as requiring election-administration review.
- Do not present examples as official voter instructions.

### Compliance Placeholder

Definition:

- Donation, campaign finance, privacy, data retention, or entity-status claims requiring review.

Examples:

- Who receives donations.
- Contribution limits or reporting.
- Refund policy.
- Privacy policy.

Requirements:

- Do not invent.
- Keep donation path as placeholder until reviewed.

## Source Hierarchy

### Highest Reliability

Use for legal, municipal, election-administration, and procedural claims:

- City of Melrose official pages.
- Melrose Charter and ordinances.
- Massachusetts General Court.
- Massachusetts Constitution and General Laws.
- Massachusetts Secretary of the Commonwealth.
- Official election administrators.

### Strong Supporting Sources

Use for implementation, civic design, and research framing:

- Center for Civic Design.
- NCSL.
- MIT Election Lab.
- RCV Resource Center.
- Peer-reviewed or academic research.

### Advocacy Sources

Use carefully for supporter framing and leads:

- FairVote.
- Local/state RCV advocacy sites.

Rules:

- Label advocacy sources.
- Do not use advocacy sources alone for legal, cost, turnout, equipment, or administrative claims.

## Citation Rules

- Every factual claim in final copy must map to a source log entry.
- Citations should be close to claims.
- Use access dates.
- Do not cite sources that were not actually reviewed.
- Do not cite a source for a claim it does not support.
- If a source supports only another jurisdiction's rule, label the jurisdiction.
- Update the source log when adding or changing claims.

## Prohibited Claims Without Verification

Melrose status:

- Melrose has adopted ranked-choice voting.
- Melrose is implementing ranked-choice voting.
- Melrose voters will use RCV in the next municipal election.
- The City of Melrose supports this campaign.
- The Melrose Elections Office approved any ballot design or explanation.

Legal pathway:

- A home rule petition guarantees RCV.
- Local approval is enough.
- State approval is a formality.
- The process will take a specific number of months.
- A specific state bill already authorizes Melrose.

Election mechanics:

- Melrose ballots will allow a specific number of rankings.
- Melrose will use single-winner RCV for all offices.
- Melrose will use proportional RCV for at-large offices.
- Results will be final on election night.
- Current voting machines are compatible.
- Recounts or audits are already solved.

Benefits:

- RCV guarantees majority winners.
- RCV eliminates spoilers in every case.
- RCV guarantees more civil campaigns.
- RCV guarantees higher turnout.
- RCV saves money.
- RCV makes elections more representative in Melrose.
- RCV reduces polarization in Melrose.

Donation/compliance:

- Donations are tax deductible.
- Contributions are subject to specific limits.
- Funds will be used for specific purposes unless reviewed and accurate.
- A specific entity receives donations unless verified.

## Balanced Concerns Policy

Concerns should be divided into:

- Clearly false misconceptions.
- Valid implementation questions.
- Evidence-mixed claims.
- Legal or administrative unknowns.

Examples:

- "Ranking a second choice hurts your first choice" can be answered as a misconception in single-winner RCV while the first choice remains continuing.
- "RCV is confusing" should be treated as a concern about voter education and ballot design, not simply a myth.
- "RCV saves money" should be treated as context-dependent, not a guaranteed benefit.

## Review Workflow For Future Copy

1. Draft page brief.
2. List every factual claim.
3. Map each claim to a source log entry.
4. Label interpretation, open question, legal-review placeholder, election-administration placeholder, or compliance placeholder.
5. Review for prohibited claims.
6. Review for partisan language.
7. Review for overclaiming benefits.
8. Review for accessibility and plain language.
9. Only then move into final copy/design.

## Update Cadence

Before public launch:

- Recheck all legal, legislative, and election-administration sources.
- Recheck RCV use/status pages because laws change.
- Confirm Melrose charter details with current official materials.
- Confirm donation/compliance status.

After launch:

- Review sources monthly during active campaign periods.
- Review after any City Council, legislative, or election-administration development.
- Review before publishing any new claim or share asset.

## Correction Policy

Future site should offer a correction path:

- Source/correction category on contact form.
- Acknowledge receipt if possible.
- Log corrections internally.
- Update source log and affected pages when verified.
- Avoid silent changes for major claim corrections; consider a changelog if public trust requires it.

## Ownership Model

Recommended roles:

- Content owner: responsible for page accuracy and tone.
- Source owner: maintains source log.
- Legal reviewer: reviews home rule and election-law claims.
- Election-administration reviewer: verifies ballot, tabulation, recount, audit, and results claims.
- Compliance reviewer: verifies donation, privacy, and campaign finance claims.
- Accessibility reviewer: reviews diagrams, forms, and plain-language clarity.

## Recommended Next Task

Create a claim matrix for each planned page before writing final website copy. The matrix should include claim text, claim category, source, citation URL, access date, review status, and publication decision.
