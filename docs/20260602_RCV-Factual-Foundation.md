## Definitions

### Ranked-Choice Voting

Verified general definition:

Ranked-choice voting is a voting method where voters rank candidates in order of preference instead of selecting only one candidate.

Sources:

- NCSL: https://www.ncsl.org/elections-and-campaigns/ranked-choice-voting
- NYC Votes: https://www.nycvotes.org/rcv
- Minneapolis Elections: https://vote.minneapolismn.gov/ranked-choice-voting/details/
- Boulder County: https://bouldercounty.gov/elections/information/ranked-choice-voting/

Caveat:

"Ranked-choice voting" is an umbrella phrase. It may refer to single-winner instant-runoff voting, multi-winner proportional ranked-choice voting, or other ranked-ballot methods. Future Melrose copy must specify the method being proposed.

### Instant-Runoff Voting

Verified general definition:

Instant-runoff voting (IRV) is a single-winner ranked voting method. Voters rank candidates. If no candidate meets the winning threshold based on first-choice votes, the lowest-ranked candidate is eliminated and ballots for that candidate transfer to the next ranked continuing candidate. The process repeats until a winner is identified under the applicable rules.

Sources:

- MIT Election Lab: https://electionlab.mit.edu/research/instant-runoff-voting
- NCSL: https://www.ncsl.org/elections-and-campaigns/ranked-choice-voting
- NYC Board of Elections: https://vote.nyc/RankedChoiceVoting
- Boulder County: https://bouldercounty.gov/elections/information/ranked-choice-voting/

Caveat:

Do not say every RCV contest is IRV. Cambridge and Portland multi-winner contests illustrate that ranked ballots can be counted in different ways.

### Proportional Ranked-Choice Voting / Single Transferable Vote

Verified general definition:

In multi-winner ranked-ballot elections, voters rank candidates and candidates are elected by reaching a threshold. Surplus votes and votes from eliminated candidates may transfer under rules designed to fill multiple seats.

Sources:

- Portland: https://www.portland.gov/vote/ranked-choice-voting
- Cambridge Election Commission: https://www.cambridgema.gov/Departments/ElectionCommission
- MIT Election Lab: https://electionlab.mit.edu/research/instant-runoff-voting

Caveat:

Future Melrose copy must not assume which method would apply to councilors-at-large or School Committee. That is a legal and policy design question.

## How Single-Winner RCV Works

Plain sequence:

1. Voters rank candidates in order of preference.
2. First-choice votes are counted.
3. If a candidate meets the winning threshold, that candidate wins.
4. If no candidate meets the threshold, the candidate with the fewest votes is eliminated.
5. Ballots that counted for the eliminated candidate move to the next ranked candidate who is still continuing.
6. Counting continues in rounds until a winner is determined under the rules.

Important wording:

- A ballot counts for one continuing candidate at a time.
- Later rankings are backup choices.
- Ranking a second choice does not take a vote away from a first choice while the first choice remains continuing.
- A voter may usually rank fewer candidates than allowed, but the consequence is that the ballot may become exhausted if all ranked candidates are eliminated.

Terms needing careful explanation:

- Continuing candidate.
- Eliminated candidate.
- Exhausted or inactive ballot.
- Overvote.
- Repeated ranking.
- Skipped ranking.
- Cast vote record.
- Preliminary vs certified results.

## Common Benefits Cited By Advocates

These are advocacy claims or research-supported possibilities, not guarantees.

### More Voter Choice

Claim:

RCV lets voters express preferences among multiple candidates rather than choosing only one.

Support:

- NYC Votes explains voters can rank up to five candidates in covered elections.
- Minneapolis explains voters can choose first, second, and third choices in municipal RCV elections.
- NCSL defines RCV as ranking candidates by preference.

Careful Melrose wording:

"Supporters argue RCV gives voters a way to indicate backup choices in multi-candidate races."

Avoid:

"Every vote counts more" or "no vote is wasted." Exhausted ballots and unranked candidates make that too broad.

### Reduced Vote Splitting / Spoiler Concerns

Claim:

RCV can reduce the pressure to choose only between the two most viable candidates because backup choices can be counted if a favorite candidate is eliminated.

Support:

- MIT Election Lab describes IRV as often discussed in relation to spoiler concerns, while noting research remains incomplete.
- FairVote strongly emphasizes this benefit from an advocacy perspective.

Careful Melrose wording:

"RCV is designed to let voters rank a favorite candidate and backup candidates in the same contest."

Avoid:

"RCV eliminates spoilers" as an absolute claim.

### Majority Or Broader Support

Claim:

RCV can elect a candidate who has majority support among continuing ballots in the final round rather than a mere plurality in the first round.

Support:

- NCSL explains the runoff analogy.
- NYC BOE and Boulder explain repeated rounds until a threshold is reached.

Caveat:

Because ballots can exhaust, the final-round winner may not always receive a majority of all ballots cast in the contest. Future copy should specify the denominator.

Careful Melrose wording:

"Single-winner RCV is intended to simulate runoff rounds without a separate runoff election."

Avoid:

"RCV guarantees a majority winner."

### Campaign Tone And Coalition Building

Claim:

Advocates argue candidates may have incentives to appeal to supporters of other candidates because second and later rankings can matter.

Support:

- FairVote makes this advocacy claim.
- MIT Election Lab notes some research points toward more civil campaign strategies but says evidence is not conclusive.
- MIT's Maine analysis found negative spending increased significantly in Maine after RCV implementation, casting doubt on simple civility claims.

Careful Melrose wording:

"Supporters hope RCV would encourage candidates to seek broader support, but research on campaign effects is mixed."

Avoid:

"RCV makes campaigns civil."

### Cost Savings From Avoided Runoffs

Claim:

In jurisdictions that otherwise hold separate runoff elections, RCV may reduce the need for a second election.

Support:

- NCSL describes RCV as similar to a runoff that can be completed with one trip to the polls.
- FairVote cites savings examples.
- MIT cost research did not find statistically significant direct cost savings or liabilities in studied local implementations.

Melrose caveat:

Melrose's current municipal election structure and preliminary election rules must be reviewed before making cost claims. If Melrose does not routinely hold separate runoffs for the relevant offices, the savings argument may be weak or inapplicable.

Avoid:

"RCV will save Melrose money."

## Common Concerns And Criticisms

### Complexity And Voter Understanding

Concern:

Ranked ballots and round-by-round tabulation are more complex than choose-one ballots.

Sources:

- MIT Election Lab says IRV is legitimate but complicated and needs more research.
- Pettigrew/Radley find improper marking rates in RCV races and raise representation questions.
- NCSL discusses implementation considerations including ballot design and voter education.

Melrose implication:

Voter education must be central, not optional.

### Ballot Marking Errors

Concern:

Voters may overvote, repeat a candidate, skip rankings, or otherwise mark ballots in ways that affect counting.

Sources:

- Boulder County and Alameda County show common errors in official voter education.
- Pettigrew/Radley report that nearly 1 in 20 voters in their RCV database improperly marked a ballot in at least one way and that RCV votes were more likely to be rejected due to improper marks than non-RCV votes.

Caveat:

Rules for handling errors vary by jurisdiction and voting system. Melrose-specific effects require election-administration review.

### Exhausted Ballots

Concern:

A ballot can become exhausted or inactive when all candidates ranked on that ballot have been eliminated or no valid next ranking remains.

Sources:

- FairVote explains that ranking only one candidate means the ballot becomes inactive if that candidate is eliminated.
- MIT Election Lab lists ballot exhaustion research as a key issue.
- NCSL implementation report discusses ballot exhaustion and voter understanding.

Careful Melrose wording:

"If a voter ranks only candidates who are later eliminated, that ballot may have no remaining candidate to count for in later rounds."

Avoid:

"The vote is thrown away" unless quoting a source. The ballot did count in earlier rounds.

### Timing And Transparency Of Results

Concern:

RCV results may take longer to explain or finalize, especially with mail ballots, close contests, or multi-round tabulation.

Sources:

- FairVote recommends preliminary round-by-round tallies, continued updates, vote total checks, full ballot record publication, visualization tools, and clear timelines.
- Center for Civic Design provides results-display guidance.
- NCSL notes results-display research is still limited.

Melrose implication:

Future campaign materials should explain how preliminary and certified results would work before adoption, not after the first RCV election.

### Equipment, Software, Audits, And Recounts

Concern:

Implementation depends on voting equipment, ballot programming, tabulation software, audit procedures, recount rules, and staff capacity.

Sources:

- FairVote lists voting-system, RCTab, audit, and recount resources.
- RCV Resource Center has pre-election, tabulation, audit, and implementation materials.
- Maine Secretary of State publishes rules and recount procedures for Maine RCV elections.
- NCSL implementation report focuses on policy implementation considerations.

Melrose implication:

Any public proposal should include a practical implementation review with the Elections Office, City Clerk, and relevant state officials.

### Equity And Accessibility

Concern:

If voter education is uneven, RCV can impose higher burdens on voters with less time, language access, disability access, or familiarity with local candidates.

Sources:

- Center for Civic Design emphasizes ballot design and accessibility.
- Alameda County and Multnomah County show multilingual and accessibility-oriented education.
- Pettigrew/Radley report higher mismarking rates in areas with more racial minorities, lower-income households, and lower educational attainment.

Melrose implication:

Accessibility and plain language should be core design requirements.

## Where RCV Is Used In The United States

Current as of source review, but this changes frequently.

Verified through NCSL:

- Alaska and Maine use RCV for statewide elections.
- RCV is used by various U.S. municipalities.
- New York City uses RCV in primary and special elections for local offices.
- Cambridge, Massachusetts and Minneapolis use ranked voting in multimember districts.
- NCSL reports that legal status varies by state, with some states permitting/requiring RCV, some prohibiting it, and some unclear.

Other official examples reviewed:

- Maine statewide/federal and primary use: Maine Secretary of State.
- NYC primary and special city-office use: NYC Votes and NYC BOE.
- Cambridge local proportional representation for City Council and School Committee: Cambridge Election Commission.
- Minneapolis municipal offices: Minneapolis Elections.
- Portland/Multnomah County city and county candidate contests: Portland and Multnomah County.
- Boulder mayoral election: Boulder County.
- Alameda County local cities: Alameda County Registrar.

Caveat:

Do not publish a national "where used" map or count without checking a current national tracker and official state/local sources immediately before launch.

## RCV History Notes

Verified from MIT Election Lab:

- Instant-runoff voting is a single-winner method often called RCV in the United States.
- Ranked voting methods have a long international and U.S. history.
- Cambridge, Massachusetts retained an IRV-like ranked system for City Council and School Committee after many earlier U.S. municipal uses had been repealed.

Melrose relevance:

- Cambridge is a valuable Massachusetts example of ranked-ballot local elections, but it is not the same as a single-winner Melrose proposal unless legal design says so.
