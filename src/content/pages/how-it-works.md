# How ranked-choice voting works

This page explains single-winner ranked-choice voting, also called instant-runoff voting. It is a general explanation, not an official Melrose ballot instruction. Melrose-specific ballot design, rank limits, write-in rules, skipped rankings, overvotes, recounts, audits, and tabulation software would need election-administration and legal review. [source: ncsl-implementation]

## 1. Voters rank candidates

On a ranked ballot, a voter can mark a first choice, second choice, third choice, and additional choices if the ballot allows. A voter usually does not have to rank every candidate, but ranking fewer candidates can affect whether the ballot has a continuing choice in later rounds. [source: boulder-rcv]

## 2. First choices are counted first

The count starts with first-choice votes. If a candidate reaches the winning threshold under the rules, that candidate wins. In many single-winner explanations, the threshold is a majority of active ballots in the final round, but the exact denominator must be described carefully because ballots can become inactive or exhausted. [source: mit-irv]

## 3. If no one reaches the threshold, counting continues

If no candidate reaches the threshold, the candidate with the fewest votes is eliminated. Ballots that were counting for that candidate move to the next ranked candidate on each ballot who is still continuing. [source: mit-irv]

## 4. Later rankings are backup choices

A ballot counts for one continuing candidate at a time. Ranking a second choice does not take support away from a first choice while that first choice remains in the count. A later ranking is a backup, not an extra vote. [source: nyc-votes-rcv]

## 5. Some ballots can become exhausted

If all candidates ranked on a ballot have been eliminated, or if no valid next ranking remains under the rules, that ballot may become exhausted or inactive in later rounds. That does not mean the ballot never counted. It means it no longer has a continuing candidate to count for. [source: ncsl-implementation]

## 6. Ballot design matters

Ranked ballots need clear instructions. Voters should be able to understand how to rank, how to avoid common marking mistakes, and what happens if they skip a ranking or mark the same candidate more than once. Those rules vary by jurisdiction, so Melrose should not copy another city's ballot instructions without official review. [source: center-civic-design-rcv]
