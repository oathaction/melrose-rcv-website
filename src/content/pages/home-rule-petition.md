# The practical path to RCV in Melrose

Melrose has not adopted ranked-choice voting. A realistic path would likely require local civic action and state authorization through a Massachusetts home rule petition. This page explains the working theory, not final legal advice.

## What is a home rule petition?

In Massachusetts, a home rule petition is a local request for the state Legislature to pass a special law for a city or town. Article LXXXIX of the Massachusetts Constitution describes how the General Court may act by special laws relating to a particular municipality, including laws based on locally filed or approved petitions. [source: ma-constitution]

## Why Melrose likely needs one for RCV

The research indicates Melrose likely needs state authorization because the Home Rule Amendment does not give cities and towns broad power to regulate elections beyond specific charter procedures. That makes election-method changes a legal-review issue, not something the campaign should present as a simple local switch. [source: ma-constitution] [source: ma-ch43b]

## What the petition would seek to authorize

A Melrose petition would likely ask the state to authorize ranked-choice voting for specified Melrose municipal elections. The petition text would need to decide or enable several issues, including:

- Which offices are covered.
- Which ranked method applies to single-seat offices.
- How multi-seat at-large offices, if included, would be handled.
- Whether a future local ballot question or voter acceptance step is required.
- How the special act interacts with the Melrose Charter and state election law.
- What implementation authority election officials would have.

Those are legal and election-administration questions. They should not be treated as settled until reviewed.

## The step-by-step pathway

1. Residents and local officials define the proposal and review the law.
2. Petition language is drafted with municipal and election-law review.
3. The Melrose City Council and any other required local actors consider the petition.
4. If locally approved for filing, Melrose's state representative or senator may file it as a bill.
5. The bill goes through the Massachusetts legislative process, including committee review.
6. If passed by the Legislature and approved by the Governor through the proper process, the special act provides the authority it states.
7. Melrose would still need implementation planning before any first RCV election.

Boston's RCV home rule petition is a useful Massachusetts example of a local proposal moving to the Legislature, but Boston's process and text do not automatically apply to Melrose. [source: ma-h4262-boston] [source: boston-rcv]

## Decisions still to be made

- Exact local approval process.
- Petition sponsor and filing path.
- Office scope.
- Counting method for each office type.
- Local voter-approval question, if any.
- Implementation timeline.
- Equipment, software, audits, recounts, costs, and voter education.

## Questions worth asking

- What would residents need to know before deciding whether to support this?
- How would Melrose protect voter understanding and accessibility?
- What would the Elections Office need to administer RCV confidently?
- What legal language would avoid confusion or unintended consequences?
