# What is ranked-choice voting?

Ranked-choice voting, often called RCV, is a way to vote when there are multiple candidates. Instead of choosing only one candidate, voters can rank candidates in the order they prefer: first choice, second choice, third choice, and so on. [source: ncsl-rcv]

The basic idea is simple: your first choice is counted first. Later choices are backup choices. In a single-winner RCV election, a later choice can matter if an earlier-ranked candidate is eliminated during the count. [source: mit-irv]

## RCV is a family of methods

"Ranked-choice voting" is an umbrella term. It can describe different systems that use ranked ballots.

For a single-winner office, RCV is often counted as instant-runoff voting. If no candidate reaches the winning threshold in the first count, the lowest candidate is eliminated and ballots can transfer to the next ranked continuing candidate. [source: mit-irv]

For multi-winner offices, ranked ballots may be counted with proportional representation or another multi-winner method. Cambridge, Massachusetts uses proportional representation for City Council and School Committee elections. That is a useful Massachusetts example, but it is not the same thing as a single-winner instant-runoff election. [source: cambridge-election]

## What RCV does not decide by itself

RCV does not automatically decide which Melrose offices would be covered, how many rankings voters would have, whether at-large offices would use the same method as single-seat offices, or how recounts and audits would work. Those questions would need legal and election-administration review before any public proposal is final.

## Why people consider it

Supporters argue RCV gives voters a way to rank a favorite candidate and backup choices without having to choose only one. They also argue it can reduce some vote-splitting pressure in crowded races. Those are reasons to consider RCV, not guarantees about every election outcome.

## Plain-language terms

- Ranked ballot: a ballot where voters can mark candidates in order of preference.
- First choice: the candidate a voter ranks first.
- Continuing candidate: a candidate still active in the count.
- Eliminated candidate: a candidate removed from later rounds under the counting rules.
- Exhausted ballot: a ballot with no valid ranked candidate left to count for in a later round.
